# Niobium

## Provenance and Naming

Niobium, a metallic element of noble bearing, deriveth its nomenclature from the Grecian nymph Niobe, renowned for her tragic tale and steadfast sorrow. Discovered in the nascent epochs of the nineteenth century by sir Charles Hatchett, this erstwhile "Columbium" found its truer name bestowed upon it in honour of the mythic figure, mirroring its own resilience amidst the crucible of change.  Its ores, concealed within the bowels of earth, are oft found commingled with tantalite, necessitating artful separation to unveil its lustrous form. 

## Physical Properties

Niobium, of atomic number forty-one, presents a silvery-white visage, gleaming with an inner radiance. Possessing a density surpassing that of common steel yet lighter than many its kin, it boasts remarkable tensile strength coupled with malleability most pleasing.  Resistant to the ravages of time and corrosion, even in the face of harsh acids and fervent heat, Niobium stands as a testament to enduring fortitude. Its melting point, a lofty 2477 degrees Celsius, speaks to its intrinsic resilience.

## Chemical Conquests

In the realm of chemistry, Niobium forms bonds with uncommon fidelity, showcasing pentavalent behaviour - a trait distinguishing it from its congeners.  Alloyed with other metals, particularly steel, it bestows enhanced strength, toughness, and resistance to heat and corrosion. This aptitude finds application in crafting superalloys for aerospace and power generation, where withstanding extreme conditions is paramount. Furthermore, Niobium's unique affinity for oxygen leads to its utilisation in creating superconducting wires, pivotal in harnessing the wonders of  cryogenics.
