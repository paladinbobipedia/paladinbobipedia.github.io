# And did those feet in ancient time

*A hymn unto Liberty, this verse doth soar,
A Paladin's heart it doth forevermore
Embrace, for 'tis a vision, bright and bold,
Of freedom's march, a tale oft told.*

##  The Prophetic Vision

At its heart, the lyric presents a prophetic tableau. "Those feet"  symbolically represent the footsteps of  Enlightened Beings,  akin to angelic messengers, traversing the hallowed lands of Albion (England) in times immemorial. They tread  upon  a liberated and ethereal Jerusalem,  a city not of brick and stone, but of spiritual emancipation and  utopian  justice. This vision speaks to a yearning for a society  freed from  oppression,  where  equity and divine  light  prevail.

##  Symbolism and Allegory

Blake's artistry blossoms in layers of  rich symbolism.  The "emancipation" alluded to  encompasses  both political and spiritual liberation.  Jerusalem,  a multifaceted emblem,  signifies  perfected human society,  a realm of  brotherhood and  the  fulfillment of  divine  purpose. The "dark Satanic Mills,"  a counterpoint  in  the larger work,  represent  the  corrupting  forces of industrialism  and  social inequality  that  stand  in  opposition  to  this  ideal.

##  Legacy and Impact

"And did those feet..."  transcended its immediate context to become a  rallying cry  for social reform and  political awakening. Its  resonance  with  movements  championing liberty  and  justice  continued  through  the  centuries,  finding  echoes in  labour rights  protests  and  the  fight  against  oppression.  Even  today,  its  message  of  a  better  world,  forged  in  freedom  and  equality,  remains  a  guiding  star  for  the  valiant  of  heart  and  mind.
